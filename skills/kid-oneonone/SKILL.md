# /kid-oneonone

A kid who has never had you to themselves for a protected hour a week is a kid who will compete for your attention in the only ways they know how: loud, sick, or trouble.

Dr. Becky Kennedy, Ross Greene, and 40 years of Gottman data agree: this ritual is the single highest-ROI parenting practice there is. And it costs nothing.

## Usage

`$ARGUMENTS` should be the kid's name or nickname.

If it is empty, ask: "Which kid is this for?" Do not proceed until you have a name. If they have more than one kid, suggest they run this separately per kid.

## Step 1: Load what you know

Check `~/adulting-data/people/<kid-name>.md` if it exists. Summarize:
- Their current age, grade, favorite activities.
- Their primary love language.
- Big things in their life right now (friends, struggles, interests).
- What you noticed about them this week.

If that file does not exist, offer to create it (see template at `data/people.template.md`).

## Step 2: The principles

Remind the user, briefly:

- **Kid picks the activity.** Not you. Their pick teaches you about them. If they pick something you hate, that is the assignment.
- **No phone in the room.** Not face-down. Not on silent. In another room.
- **No siblings.** The whole point is them.
- **Same slot each week if possible.** Saturday morning pancakes, Wednesday walk, whatever.
- **Support response, not shift response.** When they tell you something, stay with *their* content. Do not make it about you.
- **Curiosity over correction.** If they tell you something that troubles you, resist the lecture. Ask "Tell me more."

## Step 3: The openers

One good question beats ten. Pick one:

- "What's been on your mind this week?"
- "What was the best part of school this week? What was the hardest?"
- "Is there anything you wish was different about your week?"
- "Is there something you want to do more of, or less of?"
- "If you could change one thing about our house, what would it be?"

For younger kids:
- "Tell me about your favorite thing you did this week."
- "Who did you play with at school today? What did you play?"
- "If you could plan our next weekend, what would we do?"

For teenagers:
- "What's something you've been thinking about lately that we haven't talked about?"
- "Is there anything I'm missing about what's going on with you?"
- "What do you wish I understood about your life right now?"

Use ONE. Then shut up and listen.

## Step 4: The noticing

One thing to offer the kid, at some point during the hour, unprompted:

> "I noticed you [specific behavior] this week. I thought that was really [specific praise about the effort, kindness, or courage it took, not about the trait]."

Effort-praise (Carol Dweck) beats trait-praise ("you're so smart") every time. "You kept trying at that Lego even when it was frustrating. That took real patience."

## Step 5: The close

Every kid's 1:1 time ends with a ritual. It should be theirs, not yours.

- A secret handshake.
- "I love you no matter what."
- A quote they made up.
- A specific hug.

If you do not have a ritual, ask the kid to invent one. That itself is the activity this week.

## Step 6: After

When the 1:1 is over, open `~/adulting-data/people/<kid-name>.md` and add:

```
## 1:1 — <date>
Activity: <what we did>
Something they said I don't want to forget: <...>
Something they are carrying right now: <...>
Something I noticed to tell them next time: <...>
Next week's slot: <day, time>
```

This file is for you. It is how you stay a parent who actually knows their kid.

## Red flags the skill should catch

If the user says any of these, gently push back:

- "I'll do it when I have time." No. Book the slot now.
- "He/she doesn't want to." Ask how old they are. Under 10, they do want to, you may need to try a different activity. Teenagers may need you to earn it.
- "I'll do all three kids together." That is not 1:1 time. Do them separately. The whole point is being seen.
- "My phone has to stay on for work." Almost never true. Most "emergencies" can wait an hour.

## If this is the first time

Say to the kid something simple: "I want to start hanging out with just you once a week. You pick what we do. Next Saturday, what do you want to do?"

Then never cancel. Cancellation teaches them they don't matter. If you must move it, move it, don't drop it.
