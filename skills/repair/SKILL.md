# /repair

What separates secure relationships from anxious ones is not the absence of rupture. It is the presence of repair.

Every relationship will have ruptures: a snap, a yell, a dismissal, a phone-scroll while they were trying to connect, a joke at their expense. What matters is whether it gets repaired, specifically, within 24 hours.

This skill walks you through it.

## Usage

`$ARGUMENTS` should be the person. "Partner." "Son Lucas." "Mom." "Subbu."

If missing, ask: "Who is the repair for?"

## Step 1: What actually happened

Ask the user to describe it briefly. Neutrally if possible. No "but they did X first."

Capture:
- What I did or said (behavior).
- What I think the impact was on them.

If they start explaining "why they did it" (I was tired, I was stressed, they were being...), gently redirect: "That's context for you. The repair is about them. What was the impact on them?"

## Step 2: The 7-step repair

This is the protocol. It works with any close person, modified slightly for kids.

### 1. Take responsibility plainly, with no "but."

Wrong: "I'm sorry I yelled, but you were being..."
Right: "I'm sorry I yelled. That was me, not you."

Help the user draft the one sentence. It should be a clean sentence that owns the specific behavior. "I snapped at you tonight." "I talked over you in that meeting." "I got on my phone while you were telling me about your day. That was wrong of me."

### 2. Name the impact, not the intent.

"I can see that hurt you. That makes sense. You deserved better than that."

NOT: "I didn't mean to..." (This is about you.)
NOT: "I'm sorry you feel that way..." (This is not a repair, it is a deflection.)

### 3. Let the apology land. Do not rush.

Most people, after apologizing, immediately move to explain or justify. Resist.

Silence is okay. They may need 5 seconds, 30 seconds, or 5 minutes. Do not fill it.

### 4. Ask what they needed.

"What did you need from me in that moment that you didn't get?"

This question is a gift. Most people have never been asked it. They will often pause and, if they answer honestly, give you the map for the rest of the relationship.

Listen to the answer. Write it down after. Do not respond with "well but I also needed...".

### 5. Offer the structural repair.

"Next time I feel that rising in me, here is what I will try to do instead."

Specific. Observable. A behavior, not a feeling. "Next time I feel that surge of frustration, I am going to say 'I need ten minutes' and leave the room, not snap at you."

### 6. Reconnect physically (if welcomed).

For spouse: a hand, a hug, eye contact. Not sex. Not yet. Reconnection first.

For kid: a hug, a snuggle, a hand on the back. Sitting with them silently for a minute.

For friend or colleague: a handshake, a hand on the shoulder, or just warm eye contact. Match the relationship's norms.

If the person is not ready for physical contact, respect that. Do not insist. The repair does not require their affection.

### 7. (For kids only) The Three Lines.

After any rupture with a child, three sentences:

1. "I love you no matter what."
2. "You are a good kid."
3. "You didn't deserve that."

These three sentences, delivered sincerely, protect a child from internalizing the rupture as a story about themselves.

## Step 3: The structural question

Ask the user: "Is this the first time, or is this a pattern?"

If a pattern — same rupture with the same person for the third time or more — the issue is not the rupture. It is the underlying structural thing. Suggest:
- Run `/hard-conversation` about the pattern itself, once the immediate repair has landed.
- Consider therapy, couple's therapy, or coaching if this is about ongoing dynamics.

## Step 4: Deliver the repair

Do not delay. Ideally same day. Absolutely within 24 hours.

- In person is best.
- Voice call is second.
- Video call is third.
- Text is a last resort and only appropriate for minor ruptures with peers.

If you cannot do it today (for logistical reasons), at least send a brief message: "I owe you a real apology for [x]. I want to do it right, not over text. Can we talk tonight?" This holds the space.

## The underlying belief

The goal is not to never rupture. The goal is to become excellent at repair.

A parent who never yells is either lying to themselves or so emotionally shut down their kids can't reach them. The parent to be is the one who yells less over time, and repairs every time.

A partner who never disappoints is fiction. The partner to be is the one who owns their disappointment cleanly when it happens.
