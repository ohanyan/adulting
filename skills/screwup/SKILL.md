# /screwup

There is a shape to how a mature adult handles a mistake. It is the same shape whether the mistake was small (a missed deadline, a snapped tone) or large (a decision that cost the company money, a lie that damaged trust).

Four steps. In order. No skipping.

## Usage

`$ARGUMENTS` should be the screw-up in one sentence. "Missed my kid's game." "Sent a reactive email to the board." "Forgot our anniversary." "Promised a delivery I can't make."

If missing, ask for a one-sentence description.

## Step 1: Acknowledge

Plainly. To the person harmed, or the team, or the room.

Rules:
- No "but."
- No softening ("Look, I want to say a few words...").
- No PR language ("mistakes were made").
- Short, specific, declarative.

### Templates by severity

**Small:**
> "I dropped the ball on [x]. I'm sorry."

**Medium:**
> "I made the wrong call on [x]. Here's what I got wrong: [specific]. I own it."

**Large:**
> "I made a serious mistake. [Specifically what.] The impact is [what]. I am responsible. I am going to make it right in the following ways [...]."

### Anti-patterns to avoid

- "I'm sorry if you were offended." (Not an apology.)
- "I'm sorry BUT you have to understand..." (Not an apology.)
- "Mistakes were made." (Grammatical cowardice.)
- Silence and hoping it blows over. (Does not blow over. Compounds.)

Ask the user: "Write the acknowledgment sentence. Read it back. Is there a 'but' in it? Remove it."

## Step 2: Repair

Ask the harmed party (or parties) what they need.

Do not assume. Do not prescribe. Ask.

- "What would make this right, from your side?"
- "What do you need from me now?"
- "Is there anything I can do to repair the specific damage?"

Sometimes the answer is "just don't let it happen again." Sometimes it's concrete (refund a client, redo the work, send a correction to the team). Sometimes it's emotional (time, space, an acknowledgment to a wider group).

Whatever it is: do it. Specifically, within a specific timeframe. No "let me think about it."

If you cannot do what they need (you cannot un-send the email, you cannot undo the missed game), do the closest adjacent thing. And name that you cannot undo it.

## Step 3: Change the system

This is the step most people skip.

A mistake repeated is a system failure. If you did it once and apologized, congratulations, you are halfway. If it happens again because you did not change anything, the apology was performance.

Ask the user:

- What was the system, rule, calendar entry, or habit that let this happen?
- What structural change will prevent it?

Examples:
- Missed kid's game? → recurring calendar invite 2 weeks out, travel blackout 48h before, a second-chair designated at work.
- Sent a reactive email? → 24-hour rule for anything charged, draft folder.
- Forgot an anniversary? → recurring calendar alert 2 weeks out, another 1 week, another 48h with a specific action (book dinner).
- Missed a delivery promise? → don't commit to delivery dates without checking capacity, adjust process.

The change must be:
- Written down.
- Installed now, not "someday."
- Communicated to the relevant people.

## Step 4: Move on

Do not wallow.

Wallowing is self-indulgent. It centers you (the screwer-up) when the people harmed are the ones who deserve the attention. It also signals to yourself that you are the kind of person who does this, which makes you more likely to do it again.

Two rules:
1. Never apologize for the same thing twice. Once, cleanly. Then stop.
2. Shame is not discipline. Action is discipline. Get back to work.

If it haunts you for a week, run `/repair` with yourself: acknowledge (I did this), repair (I have apologized and changed the system), move on (I am the kind of person who can be trusted now because I own my mistakes, not the kind of person who does not make them).

## The Jocko principle

When something breaks, the first word out of your mouth is "Good."

Good, now I know. Good, now I can fix it. Good, now the system is better. Good, now my team has seen me own something, which makes it safer for them to own the next one.

The "Good." reframe is not denial. It is reorientation. From "this is bad, I am bad" to "this is, now what do I do with it."

## For screw-ups that involved a team or a company

Add a fifth step: **communicate the system change upward and laterally.**

Nobody trusts a leader who apologizes in private and keeps the fix in their head. If it affected a team, write a short note: "I got X wrong. Here is what happened. Here is what I am changing so it doesn't happen again." Send it.

This is how you become the kind of leader people tell the truth to.
