# /emotion-coach

When a kid is in a big feeling, your first job is not to fix the feeling. It is to be steady while they have it.

This skill is for the moment or the evening after. Use fast.

## Usage

`$ARGUMENTS` should ideally be: kid's name + one line about what is happening.

Example: `/emotion-coach Lucas he is screaming because I said no to a second ice cream`

If arguments are missing, ask: "Which kid, and what's happening?"

## Step 1: What is going on here, really

Before you act, answer silently:

- What is the *feeling* underneath the behavior? (Not the behavior. The feeling.) Is it disappointment, fear, shame, frustration, exhaustion, overwhelm?
- Is my kid dysregulated, or am I?
- Is this behavior dangerous (someone or something could be hurt), or just loud?

If you are dysregulated, pause. One round of `/reset` (physiological sigh). Your calm is the intervention. Their nervous system is co-regulating with yours.

## Step 2: Most Generous Interpretation (Dr. Becky)

Ask: **What is the most generous story about why my kid is doing this right now?**

- They are 4 and tired.
- They are 8 and had a hard day at school I don't know about yet.
- They are 13 and a peer said something cruel today.
- They are 16 and their brain is flooded with hormones and they do not know how to say what they feel.

A generous story does not excuse the behavior. It explains it. From explanation comes the right response.

## Step 3: The Gottman five steps

These are for *after* the most acute wave has passed. If they are mid-scream, skip to Step 4 and come back.

1. **Be aware of the emotion.** "You are really upset."
2. **See the emotion as a chance for connection and teaching, not an interruption.**
3. **Listen with empathy.** Do not argue, do not explain.
4. **Label the feeling.** "It sounds like you're disappointed because you were really looking forward to that." Labeling calms the amygdala. Brain imaging confirms it.
5. **Problem-solve, together, LAST.** Only after they feel heard. "What would help? What could we try next time?" Not "here's what you should have done."

## Step 4: The script for the acute moment

If they are mid-meltdown, use this structure:

### Get low.
Kneel or sit. Be at their eye level or lower.

### Name two things that are true.

Dr. Becky's core move. It sounds like:

> "You really wanted that ice cream. I know. And the answer is still no right now. Both of those things are true."

> "You are a really good kid. AND that behavior is not okay."

> "I love you. AND I won't let you hit your sister."

### Do not argue the feeling.

Wrong: "You don't need to be that upset."
Right: "Yeah, that's disappointing."

### Hold the limit. Stay soft.

Sturdy, not harsh. Warm, not permissive. The limit does not move. Your tone stays low.

### Do not threaten consequences you will not enforce.

If you said "no ice cream" you will not suddenly give ice cream because they escalated. If you did, you taught them that screaming works.

### When the wave ends, reconnect first.

A hand. A hug if they want one. "That was hard. I'm glad we got through it."

## Step 5: Repair (if you lost it)

If YOU yelled, if you threatened, if you shamed them, if you used sarcasm, if you walked away without warning: you owe a repair.

Within 24 hours, ideally the same day, use `/repair`:

> "I'm sorry I yelled. That was not about you. You didn't deserve that. I'm working on staying calm when I'm frustrated too."

Do not say: "I'm sorry I yelled, BUT you were..."

## Step 6: Later, the learning

Hours or a day later, when everyone is calm, you can have the teaching conversation.

> "What was going on for you earlier?"
> "What did you need that you didn't get?"
> "What could you try next time?"
> "What could *I* try next time?"

## Output

Append to `~/adulting-data/people/<kid>.md`:

```
## Big feeling — <date>

What happened: <...>
Feeling underneath: <...>
Most generous interpretation: <...>
What I said / did: <...>
How I wish I had responded: <...>
Repair owed: <Y/N — what>
Pattern I'm noticing across recent entries: <...>
```

The "pattern" line over time is priceless. It shows you whether this is a stage, a trigger, or an unmet need you could address structurally.

## The line to remember

Your kid will not remember what you said. They will remember whether they felt safe to have a feeling near you.
