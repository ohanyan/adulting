# /mentor-touch

Mature adults maintain connections in two directions: toward those they can teach, and toward those they can learn from.

If you only have peer friendships, your view of the world is flat. Inter-generational relationships are how you stay alive to what you don't see.

This is a monthly ritual. One touch younger, one touch older. 30 minutes each is plenty.

## The younger touch (you are the mentor)

### Who
Someone at least 10 years younger than you. A former direct report. A kid in your field. A niece or nephew who is moving into adulthood. A person you met at a conference who you felt deserved more time than you could give in the hallway.

If you do not have a younger mentee, the project for this quarter is finding one. Ask around. Tell a friend "I'm looking to mentor someone early in [their field]." People will route candidates to you.

### The principle
You are not there to give answers. You are there to:
- Ask the questions that help them see their own situation more clearly.
- Share the one story from your life that maps onto theirs.
- Open a door. A warm intro, a book, a job lead.
- Tell them you believe in them, specifically. Name the specific thing.

### The rules
- 30 minutes is enough.
- They set the agenda. Ask "what's on your mind?"
- Listen more than you talk. Aim for 70/30.
- Resist the urge to list everything you know. Give them ONE useful thing.
- End with: "Is there an intro I could make for you right now? Anyone I could send you an email about?"

### The cadence
Monthly call or coffee. Recurring calendar hold. Same person, ideally, for a year at a time.

## The elder touch (you are the listener)

### Who
Someone at least 15 years older. A parent, an aunt/uncle, a former boss, a neighbor, a retired colleague. Someone who has done what you are doing, and has come out the other side.

### The principle
You are there to ask them questions most people don't ask anymore:
- "What do you see now that you didn't see at my age?"
- "What are you proud of?"
- "What do you regret?"
- "What advice do you wish someone had given you 20 years ago that you would give me now?"
- "What do you want more of in your life these days?"

You are NOT there to update them on your life. They have heard enough about other people's lives. Make this one about them.

### The rules
- Let long pauses happen. Do not rush to fill silence. The good stuff comes after the pause.
- Take notes if that is welcome. Write down the one line from the conversation you want to remember.
- Follow up with a specific thank you. "What you said about [specific thing] really stuck with me this week."

### The cadence
Monthly if they are alive and available. Visit in person at least twice a year if possible. When they are gone, you will not have these conversations back.

## Step 1: Name the two

Ask the user:
- "Who is your mentee this quarter?"
- "Who is your elder this quarter?"

If either is blank, that is the task. Find one.

## Step 2: Schedule the next call

Open the calendar. Book the next call with each. Do not leave this skill with "I'll do it this week." Book it.

## Step 3: Prep

For each, jot:
- What is the current chapter of their life? (new job, health thing, big decision, a loss.)
- What one thing do I want to ask, or offer?
- What is the one intro or door I could open for my mentee this month?

## Step 4: After the call

Append to `~/adulting-data/people/<name>.md`:

```
## Call — <date> (mentor / elder / <duration>)

Their current chapter: <...>
What I asked: <...>
What they said that stuck: <...>
What I offered (intro, idea, resource): <...>
Action I committed to: <...>
Next call: <date>
```

## The philosophical framing

The practice of mentorship is what mature adulthood *is*. Teaching those who come after you. Listening to those who came before you. Holding both at once.

Without it, you are either a perpetual student or a self-appointed authority. Neither is adult.
