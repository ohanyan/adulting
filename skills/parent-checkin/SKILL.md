# /parent-checkin

Your parents are getting older every day you delay this.

The default pattern is: group texts, holiday calls, and a quiet feeling that you should call more often but you're busy. Then one day you're at a funeral thinking you should have asked the questions.

This skill is the fix. It takes 20 to 30 minutes a month, and it is one of the highest-compounding things you will ever do.

## Setup

- Schedule it. A recurring monthly slot on your calendar, with each parent separately if they are separated, or both together if they are together. 30 minutes.
- Do NOT do it in the car in traffic. Do it somewhere you can actually listen.
- Do it on video if you can. Voice is second best. Text does not count.

## The four questions

Ask them in order. The point is to get past "fine."

### 1. How is your health? Anything new since we last talked?

Don't accept "fine."

Follow-up if they are vague:
- "How are you sleeping?"
- "Any aches or new things bothering you?"
- "Last doctor visit, what did they say?"
- "Are you taking your meds? Any side effects?"

If they mention something new, actually listen. Do not immediately prescribe (the advice thing). Ask how it is affecting them.

### 2. What has been bringing you joy lately?

This is the question nobody asks them. Their neighbors ask them about their health. Their doctor asks about their health. You ask about joy.

If they have trouble answering, that itself is information. People who cannot name any joy are isolated. Ask what they USED to enjoy. Ask if they would do that again if someone helped.

### 3. What has been weighing on you?

Again, not "fine."

Prompts if needed:
- "Anything you've been worrying about?"
- "Anyone in the family you're thinking about a lot?"
- "Anything about the future that's been on your mind?"

Shut up and listen. Do not rush to reassure. Your reassurance is for you, not them.

### 4. What do you need from me?

Most parents have spent 30+ years not asking their kids for anything. They will say "nothing."

Push gently:
- "Anything I could help with? Errands, paperwork, a visit?"
- "Anything you've been meaning to ask and haven't?"
- "Is there something you've been wanting me to know about you that I haven't asked about?"

Whatever they say, DO IT. If you ask what they need and then don't deliver, you have taught them not to answer the question.

## Bonus: the stories

If you have any time left, ask for a story. Any story.

- "Tell me about the year you and Mom met again. I forget how that happened."
- "What was it like when you started at [their first job]?"
- "What was your dad like at my age?"
- "What are you most proud of in your life?"
- "What do you wish you'd done differently?"

Record the conversation if you can (with their permission). You will not remember these answers ten years from now. You will want to.

## Grandkid moment

If you have kids:
- Show the grandparent a photo or a video of something the kid did this week.
- Put the grandkid on for 60 seconds if they are around.
- If you are visiting, bring a drawing the kid made.

These micro-moments keep the inter-generational connection alive.

## Output

Append to `~/adulting-data/people/<parent-name>.md`:

```

## Health
<notes: anything new, meds, appointments>

## Joy
<what they named>

## Weight
<what's on their mind>

## Ask
What they asked me for: <...>
Deadline I set for myself: <...>

## Story
<anything new I learned>

## Network
Who they mentioned: <relatives, friends, neighbors>
```

This file is also your insurance for later. When they are 85 and someone asks who their doctor is, what meds they take, who their close friends are, you will know.

## The honest pushback

If the user says "my parents and I don't have that kind of relationship" or "my dad doesn't talk like that," acknowledge it, then say:

> "Start with question 2. 'What's been bringing you joy?' Nobody asks them that. The other questions can come later, once you've built the rhythm. You are not trying to have the relationship they didn't give you when you were 10. You are trying to have the relationship you can still have now."

## If a parent has already passed

This skill is for living parents. If one is gone, consider writing them a letter anyway — on their birthday, on the anniversary. Letter goes to `~/adulting-data/people/<name>-letters.md`. Not productive. Necessary.
