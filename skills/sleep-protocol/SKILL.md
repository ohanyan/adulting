# /sleep-protocol

Sleep is not a nice-to-have. It is the foundation everything else sits on.

If you are sleeping poorly, no amount of discipline, productivity hack, or supplement will compensate. And most sleep problems in adults without a clinical disorder are caused by the same 10 inputs. You can fix most of them this week.

## The diagnostic

Walk through these with the user. Honest answers only.

### 1. Morning sunlight
Do you get 2-10 minutes of direct outdoor light, eyes open (no sunglasses), within 60 minutes of waking?

This is the #1 intervention. It sets the 24-hour melatonin cycle that governs everything downstream. On cloudy days, double the duration.

### 2. Consistent wake time
Are you waking within 30 minutes of the same time, seven days a week?

Sleeping in on weekends is called "social jet lag." It is real and it degrades Monday-Thursday sleep.

### 3. Caffeine cutoff
When is your last caffeine? Huberman's protocol: nothing after 90 minutes before the midpoint of your day, practically this means no caffeine after ~2 p.m. for most people. Certainly nothing after 4 p.m.

### 4. Alcohol
Are you drinking within 3 hours of bed? Alcohol fragments sleep architecture. You will fall asleep faster and wake in the 2-4 a.m. window with cortisol spiked. Whoever told you a nightcap helps was wrong.

### 5. Bedroom temperature
Is the room 65-68°F / 18-20°C at sleep time? You need to DROP about 2°F to fall and stay asleep. A warm room wakes you at 3 a.m. without your knowing why.

### 6. Darkness
Is the room truly dark? Blackout curtains? LEDs covered? Phone face-down?

Any light on the retina, including through closed eyelids, suppresses melatonin. Light-blocking is not optional.

### 7. Screens 60 minutes before bed
Are you scrolling until lights-out? Blue light matters some, but the bigger issue is the content. Stimulating content activates the same stress systems that keep you awake.

### 8. Eating schedule
Last meal — how close to bedtime? A full stomach, especially with high-glycemic carbs, disrupts deep sleep. Aim for 3 hours before bed as a minimum.

### 9. Exercise timing
Are you doing intense cardio within 2 hours of bed? It raises core temperature at exactly the wrong time. Strength training is more forgiving. Morning or early afternoon exercise is ideal.

### 10. Wind-down ritual
Do you have a 30-60 minute transition between "day" and "sleep"? Same routine nightly — tooth brushing, shower, reading, low light. Your nervous system needs a trail of cues.

## Bonus: more subtle inputs

- **Naps.** Short (<30 min) naps before 3 p.m. are fine. Long naps or late naps destroy the sleep drive.
- **Hydration.** Too much water in the evening = bathroom wake-ups. Front-load hydration early in the day.
- **Magnesium glycinate or threonate.** 200-400 mg about an hour before bed. Most people are deficient and this helps many.
- **Hot shower 60 min before bed.** Paradoxical but works: the vasodilation drops core temp afterwards.
- **Cool down the room before bed.** If you can't run AC all night, at least have the room chilled when you get in.
- **Apnea screening.** If you snore, wake unrefreshed despite adequate hours, or your partner says you stop breathing: get tested. Apnea is massively underdiagnosed and has severe long-term consequences.

## Diagnose the pattern

Ask: which of the 10 are you getting right? Which are you getting wrong?

Prioritize fixes by expected ROI:

### High-ROI fixes
1. Morning sunlight.
2. Consistent wake time.
3. No alcohol within 3 hours of bed.
4. Cold, dark room.

### Medium-ROI fixes
5. Caffeine cutoff at 2 p.m.
6. Screens off 30-60 min before bed.
7. Last meal 3 hours before bed.

### Lower-ROI, but add if the above are fixed
8. Wind-down ritual.
9. Exercise timing.
10. Magnesium supplementation.

## Action plan

Pick the top 3 fixes. One per week. Do not try all 10 at once; you will change nothing.

## When to get help

If after 4 weeks of honest compliance with the protocol, sleep is still poor:
- Get an apnea screening.
- Rule out thyroid, ferritin, B12 issues (request labs).
- Consider CBT-I (cognitive behavioral therapy for insomnia). Evidence > Ambien.
- Talk to a sleep doc.

Sleep is the foundation. Get it right.
