# /shutdown-ritual

From Cal Newport's *Deep Work*. The point is not to finish work, it is to stop. Cognitively stop. The ritual is how your mind learns to release.

## Step 1: Inbox sweep

Walk through in order:

1. Email inbox. Any unread? Either reply in under 2 minutes, schedule a reply for tomorrow, or capture into the task system.
2. Slack / Teams / chat. Same rule.
3. Text messages that are work-adjacent.
4. Browser tabs. Close them or save to read-later.
5. Physical desk. Papers into piles or away.

You are not trying to reach zero. You are trying to reach *captured*. Nothing important rattling around in your head unlogged.

## Step 2: Task system review

Ask the user:
- "Any open loops from today that you have not captured yet?"
- "Any promises you made today that need to be on a list?"
- "Anyone waiting on you that you need to follow up with tomorrow?"

Add each to their task system.

## Step 3: Tomorrow's top 1

> "If you only get one thing done tomorrow, what is it?"

One sentence. Not three. Write it where you will see it first thing.

## Step 4: Calendar glance

Look at tomorrow's calendar. Any meeting that needs prep? If so, block 15 minutes before it and label the block with the prep task. Don't do the prep now. Just guarantee the space.

## Step 5: The phrase

The user says out loud: **"Schedule shutdown complete."**

This is not a joke. The phrase is a conditioned trigger. Over time your brain learns: after those three words, work is done.

## Step 6: The commitment

One evening rule, pick any:

- No work email after shutdown.
- No work Slack after shutdown.
- Phone stays in a drawer until morning.
- If a work thought arrives, write it on a pad and release it.

## Output

Append a one-line log to `~/adulting-data/journal/shutdown-log.md`:

```
<date> <time>  top1: <sentence>  status: shutdown complete
```

Print back a clean summary. End with: "Go be with the people in the house."
