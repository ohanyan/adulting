# /gratitude

Gratitude practice works. The research is consistent across decades. But most of what people call a gratitude practice is the cheap version, and the cheap version does very little.

The cheap version: "I'm grateful for my family, my health, and my job." Fine. Does basically nothing neurologically after the first few weeks, because it is vague and repetitive.

The effective version has three features.

## The three rules

### 1. Specific

Not "my partner." Name the specific thing they did today or this week. Specific enough that another person reading it would be able to picture the moment.

### 2. Person-linked

Not "I'm grateful for coffee." "I'm grateful that Elena makes coffee for me most mornings even though she doesn't drink it." Attached to a person whose action made the good thing possible.

### 3. Why-linked

Not just "Elena made me coffee." But also "...which lets me skip 5 minutes of friction every morning when I'm already stressed about the day." What about the thing matters to you, and why.

## Daily form

Every day, three items.

Template:

```
1. Today I'm grateful that <specific thing> that <person> did, because <why it mattered to me>.
2. Today I'm grateful that <specific thing> that <person> did, because <why it mattered to me>.
3. Today I'm grateful that <specific thing> that <person> did, because <why it mattered to me>.
```

Some of the three can be about yourself (you did something hard, you were kind to yourself, you tried). Some can be about circumstances or nature. But at least ONE per day should be person-linked to someone in your life.

## The extra step (weekly)

Once a week, pick one item from the week's gratitude log and TELL THE PERSON.

- Text them the specific thing, with the why.
- Mention it in person.
- Write a short note.

This is the part that transforms a private practice into a relational one. Told gratitude is connection. Untold gratitude is just mood management.

## Examples

### Low-value (don't do this)

- "Grateful for my family."
- "Grateful for my health."
- "Grateful for my job."

### Medium-value (better)

- "Grateful my wife made dinner tonight."
- "Grateful my team shipped the release."
- "Grateful the weather was nice."

### High-value (do this)

- "Grateful Elena made dinner tonight even though she had a harder day than I did, which freed me to have a real conversation with Lucas about his friend problem instead of managing the kitchen."
- "Grateful Subbu pushed back on my first draft of the investor memo, because it saved me from sending a weaker version and hurting our credibility with that fund."
- "Grateful for the long walk at sunset because I noticed how much I'd been stuck in my head this week and needed the 45 minutes outside."

## Output

Append to `~/adulting-data/journal/gratitude-YYYY-MM.md`:

```
## <date>
1. <specific, person-linked, why>
2. <specific, person-linked, why>
3. <specific, person-linked, why>

(weekly) Told <name> about <item>: <Y/N, when>
```

## The honest caveat

If you have a streak of hard days or are moving through grief, gratitude practice can feel false. It isn't. You can be both heartbroken and grateful that someone brought you coffee. Naming the small good things does not deny the big hard thing. It keeps you from drowning in it.

If the practice feels impossible, drop to ONE item, for any person or thing, and that is plenty.
