# /partner-checkin

The research is unambiguous. Gottman's 40+ years of couples work lands on one pattern: couples who thrive have structured weekly contact that is not about logistics. Not kids, not calendar, not groceries. *Them.*

This is that ritual.

## Setup

- 30 minutes minimum, ideally 45.
- Same day and time each week if possible. Friday night, Sunday morning, whatever is yours.
- Phones off or in another room. Not face-down. Away.
- Not in bed. Somewhere you sit up.
- Drinks are fine. Screens are not.

## The structure

### 1. Appreciation (2 minutes)

Each of you shares one specific appreciation from the week. "Thank you for handling bath time Tuesday when I was on that call. I know it wasn't your turn."

Specific beats general. Behavior beats trait. ("You are a great partner" is worse than "You picked up the dry cleaning without me asking.")

### 2. High of the week, low of the week (5-10 minutes)

Each of you, in turn, shares:
- The high.
- The low.

No problem-solving. No advice. The other person says something like: "That sounds heavy. Tell me more."

### 3. What are you carrying? (5-10 minutes)

The real question. Ask:

> "What is on your mind this week that I might not know about? Work, family, friends, health, the kids, us. Anything."

Listen without responding. When they finish, use Kate Murphy's support response: stay with *their* content. Do not shift to your content. Ask one curiosity question: "What was that like?" or "What do you make of that?"

Then they ask you the same question. You answer.

### 4. Us (5-10 minutes)

Two questions, taken seriously:

- "What did I do this week that made you feel loved?"
- "What did I miss?"

The "miss" question is the one most couples skip. It is the one that matters most. It should be answered with one specific thing, not a list of grievances. "When I came home Wednesday and you were on your phone for the first 10 minutes, that felt like I didn't matter."

The receiver's only response is: "Thank you for telling me. I hear you. I'm sorry. How can I repair that?"

No defense. No explanation. No "but."

### 5. Anything unsaid (3 minutes)

> "Is there anything between us right now that we haven't named?"

If yes, use Brené's rumble language: "The story I'm telling myself is..." Speak from the story, not the accusation. If it needs more than 10 minutes, schedule a dedicated time and use `/hard-conversation`.

### 6. One thing for this week (2 minutes)

Each of you names one thing you will do for the other this week. In their love language (see `/love-map-refresh`).

Write it down. Put it on the calendar if it needs a calendar.

### 7. Close

A 20-second hug. Not a side hug. A real one.

## The rule about logistics

Logistics can have 10 minutes at the END if needed. Never the start. Never the whole check-in. If logistics try to eat the slot, stop, and schedule a separate 15-minute "ops meeting" for the week.

## Output

After the check-in, optionally log to `~/adulting-data/journal/partner-checkin-YYYY-MM-DD.md`:

```
# Partner check-in — <date>

## Appreciations
Mine: <...>
Theirs: <...>

## Carried this week
Mine: <...>
Theirs: <...>

## The miss
What they said I missed: <...>
My repair: <...>

## This week for them
<specific action, day>

## Unsaid
<anything parked for later>
```

This journal is private. Never share it without their consent.

## Before the first one

If you have never done this and your partner might think it's weird, don't spring it. Tell them: "I want to try something. A weekly 30-minute sit-down where we actually talk about us. No phones, no logistics. Would you be up for trying it for a month?"

If they say no, ask what version they would say yes to. Meet them there.
