# /friend-reach-out

Adult friendships don't decay because you stopped caring. They decay because nobody scheduled them.

The research on adult male friendship is grim: sharp decline after 30, most close friendships lost by 50. The research on female friendship is better but not immune, especially after kids. The fix is not heroic effort. It is a small, scheduled, specific touch.

This skill makes that touch happen.

## The three tiers

If you have never set up your people registry, walk the user through this first. Ask them to list:

**Tier 1 (5-10 people):** Inner circle. The people you would call at 3 a.m. or from a hospital. Includes best friends, best man/maid of honor, siblings-as-friends.

**Tier 2 (15-30 people):** Close friends. People whose lives you want to stay in. People you have been meaningful to, and who have been to you.

**Tier 3 (50-200 people):** Network. People you like and respect but do not need weekly contact with. Old colleagues, former teammates, people from a class, people from a stage of life.

Save to `~/adulting-data/people/friendship-tiers.md`.

## The cadences

- **Tier 1:** Every 2 weeks, some kind of real touch. Voice memo, call, in-person. Not a meme.
- **Tier 2:** Monthly. A real message, referencing something specific from last time.
- **Tier 3:** Quarterly. A 5-minute favor. An article. An intro. A happy birthday with an actual sentence.

## Step 1: Surface who is overdue

Ask the user: when was the last time you had real contact with each Tier 1 person?

For each:
- Name.
- Last real contact (date or roughly).
- Overdue? Y/N.

You can infer from text message history if they share it with you, or just ask. Sort by most overdue.

## Step 2: Draft the message

For each overdue Tier 1 person, help the user draft a message that is NOT:
- "Thinking of you" with no specifics.
- "We should catch up soon" with no proposed time.
- A meme.
- A reply to their most recent Instagram story.

What it SHOULD be:
- References something specific you know about their life right now.
- Asks a real question, not "how are you."
- Proposes something concrete if they are local. A specific time for a walk, a coffee, a game.

### Template

> "Hey [name], I was just thinking about [specific memory or recent thing in their life]. [Something you know about what they're dealing with — work change, new kid, health thing — and a real question about it]. Also, are you around [specific day]? I'd love to [specific activity, specific time]."

### Examples (adapt to the friend)

- "Hey M, I was just thinking about that terrible burrito place we ended up at after Jenna's wedding. How's the new job treating you? You mentioned the commute was brutal. Are you around Saturday? I want to go for a real walk, 90 minutes, just us."

- "Hey R, your mom crossed my mind today, I know the anniversary was last week. How are you actually doing with that? If you want to talk or just sit in a car and drive somewhere, I'm free Sunday."

- "K, I know you've been deep in the launch. I'm not texting for an update. I'm texting to say I'm proud of you. And also: want to do the ridge hike on May 17th? I'll drive."

## Step 3: For the ones you haven't talked to in 90+ days

Use the re-engage opener:

> "I was just thinking about [specific memory — a trip, a job, a kid moment, a meal]. How are you, actually?"

Do not apologize for the gap. Do not make it a thing. Just open the door and walk through.

## Step 4: Schedule the actual thing

A message is not a friendship. A friendship is scheduled time.

If they say yes, put it on the calendar IMMEDIATELY. Not "let's find a time next week" (that time will never be found). Propose the time. Send the invite.

## Step 5: The quarterly 5-minute favor (Adam Grant)

For Tier 2 and 3, once a quarter, do a 5-minute favor for someone:
- An intro that would matter to them.
- An article or podcast you think they'd love, with a line about why.
- A public endorsement (LinkedIn, a review, a word to a mutual connection).
- A birthday note with an actual sentence.

Cost: 5 minutes. Compounding value over a decade: enormous.


## The line to close with

Remind the user: "Nobody on their deathbed regrets having stayed close to the people they loved."
