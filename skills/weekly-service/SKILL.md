# /weekly-service

One of the markers of mature adulthood across almost every moral tradition is: a share of your time and attention flows to people who are not obligated to repay you.

Not your family. Not your customers. Not your boss. Someone outside the exchange.

This does not require grand gesture. It requires 15 to 60 minutes a week, directed outward.

## Usage

Run once a week, ideally on Sunday evening or Monday morning, to plan one act for the coming week.

If the user has not done this before, start with the smallest possible version and build.

## The menu

### Small (15-30 min)

- Write a detailed LinkedIn recommendation for someone who deserves one.
- Make an intro. A real one, with a thoughtful paragraph about why each person should meet.
- Send a specific thank-you note (email or paper) to someone whose work mattered to you.
- Send a podcast, article, or book recommendation with a line about why it made you think of them.
- Write a detailed review on behalf of a small business (restaurant, local shop, contractor) that did good work.
- Check in with someone who is going through something hard with no agenda.

### Medium (30-60 min)

- Mentor call with someone early in their career. (See `/mentor-touch`.)
- Volunteer shift at a local organization.
- Host a meal for friends, especially one that includes someone new to the group.
- Help a friend or colleague move, paint, assemble, fix.
- Donate and deliver items to a shelter, food bank, or specific person in need.
- Make a significant introduction and actually broker it (not just cc, but follow up until they connect).

### Larger (90+ min, monthly or quarterly)

- Serve on a board or committee.
- Coach a kid's team.
- Teach a workshop at a school or community program.
- Drive a friend to a medical appointment.
- Anonymous giving — time, money, or referral, with no credit attached.

## The rules

### 1. Not for credit

If you need it seen to do it, it is not service. It is signaling.

You can post about it. You can tell a friend. That is fine. But the test is: would you still do it if nobody saw? If not, pick something else.

### 2. Not for family

Family care is good, necessary, and required. It is not "service" in this sense. This skill is about the attention that flows beyond the ones who are already in your care.

### 3. Not optimization

Do not spreadsheet your service. Do not count it toward a "giving plan" that makes you feel better at year-end. The moment service becomes accounting, it stops working.

### 4. Not transactional

You are not hoping this helps you business-wise. If it does, that is a welcome side effect. If it does not, the act still did its work.

### 5. Specific, not vague

"Be more generous" is not a plan. "Write a LinkedIn recommendation for James this Tuesday night" is.

## Step 1: Pick the week's act

Ask the user: "What are you doing this week, outside your family, for someone not obligated to you?"

If they are stuck, offer the menu. Pick ONE. Specific day and time if possible.

## Step 2: Do it

That's it. No fanfare.

## Step 3: Log, briefly

```
~/adulting-data/journal/service-log.md

# <week-of>
What: <...>
For: <person or group>
Time: <minutes>
How I felt after: <one line>
```

The "how I felt after" line is not for show. Over months, it teaches you which kinds of service actually nourish you versus which kinds drain you. Optimize (quietly, for yourself) toward the kinds that keep you wanting to do more.

## The philosophical note

The concentric circles of adult responsibility are: self, partner, kids, parents, close friends, extended family, community, strangers, the world.

A mature adult does NOT skip the inner circles for the outer ones (the person who ignores their own kids while volunteering for strangers is broken in a specific way). But they also do not stop at the inner circles and let the rest rot.

Weekly service is the small tax you pay outward, so the concentric circles stay open.

## The line to end on

Service is not charity you owe. It is the tuition for living in a working society. Pay it weekly in small amounts, and the full bill never comes due at once.
