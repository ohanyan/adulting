# /reset

Do not keep typing. Do this first.

## Step 1: Physiological sigh (60 seconds)

Two sharp inhales through the nose, one long exhale through the mouth. Repeat three times.

This is not meditation. It is a specific breathing pattern that downregulates the sympathetic nervous system in roughly one minute. Andrew Huberman's Stanford lab has it as the fastest voluntary stress intervention known.

Do it with the user. Count the rounds.

## Step 2: The Jocko "Good." reframe

> "Say the word 'Good.' out loud. Then answer: what does this situation make possible? What can I now learn, fix, or prevent?"

Jocko's point: if you tell yourself *good* about every bad thing, eventually you stop flinching. You stop being shocked that the world broke. You start looking for the move.

## Step 3: The ownership check

Three questions, one sentence each:

1. **What part of this is mine?** (Not "whose fault is it" — what part of the outcome was inside my control and I missed it?)
2. **What part is not mine?** (And therefore I can stop ruminating on it.)
3. **What is the next right action in the next 10 minutes?**

## Step 4: The delay rule

If this is about a message you are about to send (email, text, Slack): do not send it for 60 minutes. Save as draft. Come back.

If this is about a decision you are about to make: sleep on it. Real 24 hours. Almost no decision is worse for being made tomorrow.

## Step 5: If the stakes are the people you love

One extra question: **"Will I be proud of how I acted here in a week?"**

If no, do not act yet. The moment is lying to you about its importance.

## Output

One-line log to `~/adulting-data/journal/reset-log.md`:

```
<date> <time>  trigger: <what>  next action: <what>  sent/decided: <Y/N>
```

If the user said "yes, I sent it anyway" or "yes, I snapped" — gently suggest `/repair`.
