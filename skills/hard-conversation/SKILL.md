# /hard-conversation

If you are avoiding a conversation, you are having it anyway. The version you are having is the one in your head, alone, at 2 a.m., with the worst version of the other person.

This skill takes you through structured prep. 15 minutes, tops. The payoff is huge.

## Usage

`$ARGUMENTS` should be: who and what. "With my partner about the trip to her parents'." "With my cofounder about his direct reports." "With my dad about his drinking."

If missing, ask: "Who is this with, and what is it about?"

## Step 1: Regulate first

Before any prep, a 3-minute regulation check. If the user is activated right now, this prep will get warped.

- Run 3 rounds of physiological sigh (see `/reset`).
- Drink water. Walk 5 minutes if possible.
- Come back when the adrenaline has left the body.

Do not skip this.

## Step 2: The observation (not evaluation)

Rosenberg (Nonviolent Communication): observations are what a video camera would see. Evaluations are what your mind added.

Ask the user: "Describe what happened in a way a neutral observer would agree with. No interpretations, no 'always' or 'never', no character judgments."

Wrong: "You're always on your phone at dinner."
Right: "The last three dinners together, the phone was in your hand for most of the meal."

Wrong: "He's being passive-aggressive."
Right: "He hasn't replied to my last two direct questions and responded to a third by CC-ing his manager."

Capture this one observation.

## Step 3: The feeling (not the thought)

Many "I feel..." statements are thoughts in disguise:
- "I feel attacked" — thought. (Real feeling: hurt, afraid, angry.)
- "I feel ignored" — thought. (Real feeling: lonely, sad, invisible.)
- "I feel like you don't care" — thought. (Real feeling: scared, alone.)

Ask the user: "What is the underlying feeling, named as a feeling?"

Use real feeling words: hurt, lonely, scared, angry, disappointed, ashamed, anxious, exhausted, powerless, sad.

## Step 4: The need

Behind every hard feeling is an unmet need. Ask: "What do you need here? Underneath the complaint, the reaction, the irritation — what is the actual need?"

Common ones:
- Respect
- Predictability
- Partnership / not being alone in this
- Rest
- Trust
- Honesty
- Autonomy
- To matter
- Safety

## Step 5: The request

Requests must be:
- Specific (not vague).
- Doable (in the person's power).
- Present-tense (not "you should have").
- Answerable with yes or no.

Wrong: "Be more supportive."
Right: "When I come home late from work, could we skip the phone for the first 15 minutes and just sit together?"

Wrong: "Stop being controlling."
Right: "I'd like to make the call on my own work schedule without checking in first. Would you be willing to try that for a month and see how it goes?"

A real request survives a "no." If you cannot hear "no," you are making a demand, and the other person will feel it.

## Step 6: The most generous interpretation

Dr. Becky's move, adapted for adults.

Ask the user: "What is the most generous story about why this person is doing what they are doing?"

Not an excuse. An explanation. Examples:
- "He is scared of losing his role, not trying to hurt me."
- "She has been sleeping badly and is running on empty."
- "My dad is afraid of being forgotten, and the only way he knows to stay central is to make decisions for everyone."

If you can enter the conversation holding a generous story about them, you will not need to "win." You will need to understand.

## Step 7: The story I'm telling myself

Brené Brown. Write it down: "The story I'm telling myself is..."

- "The story I'm telling myself is that you don't care about me anymore."
- "The story I'm telling myself is that you think I'm failing at my job."
- "The story I'm telling myself is that I am less important to you than your work."

This is NOT what you say to them as a fact. It is what you say to them as a story you are running, to own that it is YOUR story.

In the conversation: "The story I'm telling myself is [...]. Is any part of that true?"

This is one of the most disarming phrases in the English language. It transforms accusations into something both people can look at together.

## Step 8: The one-sentence opener

Write the single sentence you are most afraid to say. That is your opener. Clear is kind. Unclear is unkind.

Examples:
- "I don't feel close to you right now, and I want to."
- "I think you're drinking more than is good for you, and I love you too much not to say it."
- "I think we've been pretending this will work out, and I don't think it's going to."
- "I don't trust this decision, and I need you to hear why before you make it."

If the sentence scares you, you probably have the right one.

## Step 9: The outcome you actually want

Two options. Pick one.

- **Connection.** I want us to understand each other and keep moving together.
- **Being right.** I want them to admit I was correct.

If you pick the second, do not have the conversation. You will not get what you want anyway, and you will damage something in the process.

## Step 10: The logistics

- Where and when? Not at the end of an already exhausted day. Not in the car with kids in the back.
- A walk can be better than a face-to-face. Side-by-side lowers the temperature.
- How long do you have? (Guard against the conversation running into something else and getting cut off at the worst moment.)
- Phones away.

## Before the user goes

End with a single question: "After doing this, do you still need to have this conversation this week, or do you now see it differently?"

Sometimes the prep itself changes the answer. Either way, the prep was worth it.
